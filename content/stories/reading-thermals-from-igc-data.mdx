Every thermal in your IGC file leaves a signature. The question is whether you know how to read it.

Parametrics breaks each thermal into three core metrics: **climb rate**, **centering score**, and **commit delay**. Together they tell a more complete story than any single number can.

## Climb Rate

Average climb rate across the full thermal is the most visible metric, but it's often the least useful on its own. A thermal with a 2.5 m/s average might reflect genuinely weak lift — or it might reflect poor centering that kept you on the edge of a 4 m/s core.

That's why climb rate only becomes meaningful when read alongside centering score.

## Centering Score

Centering score measures how efficiently you found and stayed in the strongest part of the lift. It's derived from the variance in your climb rate within the thermal — a tight, consistent climb suggests you were close to the core; a noisy, variable one suggests you were hunting.

A high climb rate with a low centering score is a warning sign: you found strong air but couldn't hold it. A moderate climb rate with a high centering score is often more encouraging — it means you were flying the thermal well, just in a weaker column.

## Commit Delay

Commit delay measures the time between entering detectable lift and beginning to circle. It's one of the more revealing metrics in the dataset because it's almost always longer than pilots expect.

A few seconds of delay is normal — you need to assess the lift before committing. But delays over 10–15 seconds in usable lift typically indicate hesitation, and they show up clearly when you compare your commits across a full flight.

Pilots who improve their commit delay often do so not by circling earlier in everything, but by getting better at distinguishing real lift from turbulence quickly — reducing the assessment window without increasing false commits.

## Reading Them Together

The most useful frame is to look at all three for each thermal and ask: where did the time go?

- High delay, good centering, good climb → you took your time committing but flew it well once in
- Low delay, poor centering, poor climb → you committed fast but couldn't find the core
- High delay, poor centering, decent climb → you hesitated, then circled on the edges of something better

Over a full flight, patterns emerge. Over a full season, they become your profile.
